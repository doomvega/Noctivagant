Remote Editing and the Simplicity of Webmastery

Recently, I've expanded my efforts to move away from social media. This should not surprise anyone; pretty much my entire deal since figuring out webmastery was taking back the web from corporate hands. I've done some really neat things lately, mostly in the realm of making my websites easier to work on. (Some of it was a pain in the ass, but it was worth it.)

Noctivagant's blog operates like this:

  1. I write posts by hand, directly into HTML.
  2. I manually update an RSS feed.

It's incredibly easy, allows for me to write whatever I want, and feels home-made. I love doing it.

Doing this directly into the Neocities editor is fine, but it feels a little janky to me. I should be able to connect to my website's code remotely and edit from any computer available to me, without needing to manually update a GitHub repo every time I do so. Also, I'm really tired of needing my tower to do anything with my website. Most of what I've built for Noctivagant, I've built on my tower. No big deal, but when you're always in pain and always exhausted, it's so much easier to just flip open a laptop and get coding.

Luckily, there are ways to do this.

You can hook up VSCode to GitHub. This system works best if you don't have an existing GitHub repository for your project. (I had to remake a ton of repos for all of the sites I maintain.) If you do have an existing repo, you can connect to it using VSCode's "connect to..." function. Just paste the URL of your repo and it should pull the code for you to tinker with. You can then save this as a workspace so you can open and alter whenever.

Pushing updates from GitHub to Neocities is a little bit more technical (and certainly annoying if you have a ton of sites like I do), but it takes only a few minutes and some testing to make sure your updates are getting pushed correctly. You should be able to add this system to an existing repo, but I've found that it's easier to just make a new repo, just like with the system above.

This means that updating my blog and RSS feed are simultaneous rather than one after the other, like is usual in the Neocities code editor. I've found this system to be smooth, rewarding, and relatively easy to implement, and it makes the process of tending to my sites much simpler. It's actually made me reconsider some of my blogging spots. I love BearBlog, but doing this is cool too. I'm not sure what to do here if the goal is trimming down my social media! And don't even get me started on Zonelets. I've been considering making a Zonelets blog, but then what would I do with this one? Would I just archive and replace it? Zonelets is so fun to work with remotely...

All of this is to say making websites is awesome, and if you can get a system going that makes working on your sites a little easier despite the horrors, you should do it. I'm not even joking when I say that working on my sites like this has made me vastly reconsider my social media usage in its entirety. (This is partly because of my experiences on Cohost‚Äî I found very quickly that detaching myself from numbers and metrics made me feel a lot better about the Internet at large.)

I wonder if I could make my BlueSky and Mastodon accounts totally automated? We'll see.

Pruning and Changing

The key to having a healthy garden is a process called "deadheading", which is where you identify parts of a plant that are dying and gently remove them. I have done this countless times in my gardens. My mother taught me how to do it. I've always felt bad about it, as if I was hurting the plant by removing dead leaves and flowers and branches. But seeing my plants recover, every time, was worth the worry.

Once again, I have deleted two of my Neocities sites‚Äî Felis Celesti and PAST SELF, both projects I started as a budding webmaster that I simply have not had the time or energy to work on. They were dying flowers. I plucked them away. These things happen, and I have to remind myself time and time again that it's okay. Change is a natural part of life. It will be okay.

I love making websites, but it can get difficult to maintain so many, especially when they have no audience‚Äî including yourself. Noctivagant has been for me, fundamentally, and no one else‚Äî though I am grateful that so many other webmasters love it too. I don't want it to sound like I'm trying to negate or invalidate that love. Thank you for loving my work.

I'm going to shift my focus, because I have to in order to survive. In the future, if I'd like to come back to those sites, I will. For now, though, I'm going to work on finishing my Java studies, getting commissions, and honing my webdev skills by working on the sites that make the most sense. Right now, that's Noctivagant, my professional site, the Artist's Resource, and the Testing Range. I still have other sites, of course, and some system members have spaces on Neocities. We are wondering about building our own small blog system, like this one, that can be sorted by tag.

And Now I'm Rambling

I talked about this on BearBlog, but I didn't put it quite clearly‚Äî I think I have too many spaces online. Genuinely. I am not a very extroverted person, and while I do like connecting with folks, I would rather connect with them through excited exchanges about cool websites and code rather than through recommendations made by an algorithm that thinks accounts with enormous following lists are real. (Seriously, Bluesky‚Äî you have a bot problem.)

I like BearBlog, and of course I like Neocities. Mastodon is a good place to find art, but it is also prone to flaws. (You people have got to stop complaining about Bluesky and Windows. I'm on an art-based instance! I can see it from here! Knock it off!) And beyond those three, that's really all that I like. Other social spaces are weird and disconnected for me. I know that YouTube and Bluesky have a lot of potential, and I could grow my audience that way, but those are not spaces I want to be in and I think that's okay.

I left some other Fedi servers recently, and I'm not feeling much of a loss at all. I was absolutely spreading myself out too thin. Even with PostyBirb, I found myself switching between socials every five minutes‚Äî do I have notifications here? Did someone respond to me? How has the timeline been? And so on, and so forth, between multiple sites.

Personally, we don't need much, hence all the socmed and site pruning. I'm thinking about either upgrading my BearBlog to support the dev, or getting down to it and writing a site that uses a searchable tag system to act as a system blog. I've tried to be public about our plurality before, and I am still incredibly hesitant about it, but I think it would be a good project to write, and we would open-source the code so anyone could use it. Of course, that means learning more JavaScript... but I think that's a good price to pay to move away from other sites that offer no control whatsoever.

I think a lot of us are sad right now. There's a lot going on in the world that is out of our control. But this makes me feel like I have some grip on reality, and I recommend that you do the same. Deadhead your garden. Make room for new ideas to flourish.
